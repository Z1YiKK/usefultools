Compound Interest: The Mathematical Engine Behind Wealth Building

📅 June 14, 2026  ·  ⏱️ 7 min read  ·  📚 4 references

Einstein reportedly called compound interest the "eighth wonder of the world." Whether or not he actually said it, the math behind the claim is undeniable. Here's why.

The Formula That Changed Finance

The compound interest equation is deceptively simple:

A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)

Where A = final amount, P = principal, r = annual rate, n = compounding frequency, t = years. The magic is in the exponent — as t grows, growth becomes exponential, not linear.

The Rule of 72: A Mental Shortcut

Divide 72 by your annual interest rate to get the approximate number of years to double your money. At 7% (historical S&P 500 real return), money doubles every ~10.3 years. This rule, first documented by Luca Pacioli in 1494, remains one of the most useful mental math tools in finance.

📖 Pacioli, L. (1494). Summa de Arithmetica, Geometria, Proportioni et Proportionalita. Venice.

Starting Early vs. Starting Big

Someone investing $200/month from age 25 to 35 (10 years, $24,000 total) and then stopping will have more at retirement than someone investing $200/month from age 35 to 65 (30 years, $72,000 total). At 7%: the early starter ends with ~$460,000 vs. the late starter's ~$244,000. Time in the market beats timing the market.

📖 Malkiel, B. G. (2023). A Random Walk Down Wall Street (13th ed.). W. W. Norton.

The Dark Side: Compound Debt

Compound interest works both ways. Credit card debt at 22% APR compounds against you. A $5,000 balance making minimum payments ($125/month) takes 5+ years to pay off and costs over $3,000 in interest. This is why understanding compounding isn't optional — it's financial literacy 101.
