The Mathematics of Mortgage Amortization: Why Extra Payments Work

📅 June 8, 2026  ·  ⏱️ 8 min read  ·  📚 4 references

Many homeowners make extra mortgage payments without understanding why they're so powerful. The answer lies in the amortization formula — and once you see the math, you'll never look at your mortgage the same way.

The Amortization Formula Deconstructed

The standard fixed-rate mortgage payment formula is:

M = P × [r(1+r)^n] / [(1+r)^n − 1]

Where M = monthly payment, P = principal, r = monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12), and n = total number of payments.

What this formula hides is that interest is front-loaded. In the first year of a 30-year, $300,000 mortgage at 6.5%, approximately 80% of every payment goes to interest. By year 15, that drops to ~55%. By year 25, it's ~25%.

Why Early Extra Payments Are So Powerful

Every extra dollar you pay toward principal in Year 1 reduces the balance that would have accrued interest for 29 more years. That single dollar saves you:

Savings = $1 × (1 + r)^remaining_months − $1

At 6.5% over 29 remaining years: one extra dollar in Year 1 saves approximately $5.55 in interest over the life of the loan. Applied to $200/month extra: that's ~$97,000 in interest saved on a $300,000 loan.

📖 Fabozzi, F. J., & Modigliani, F. (2009). Mortgage and Mortgage-Backed Securities Markets. Harvard Business School Press. See Chapter 3 for the full amortization derivation.

The Diminishing Returns Curve

Extra payments become less impactful over time — not because they're less valuable, but because there are fewer remaining compounding periods. The relationship between "month of extra payment" and "interest saved" follows an exponential decay curve.

A $1,000 extra payment in Year 1 might save $5,500 in interest. The same $1,000 in Year 20 saves only about $800. This is why financial advisors universally recommend making extra payments as early as possible.

📖 Brueggeman, W. B., & Fisher, J. D. (2011). Real Estate Finance and Investments (14th ed.). McGraw-Hill. Chapter 4 covers loan amortization mathematics in detail.

The Bi-Weekly Payment Hack — Explained

Paying half your mortgage every two weeks results in 26 half-payments = 13 full payments per year instead of 12. On a $300,000 loan at 6.5%, this alone cuts ~5.5 years off the term and saves ~$77,000 in interest.

Why? Because that 13th payment goes entirely to principal every year — accelerating the amortization curve every single year.

Inflation: The Hidden Ally of Fixed-Rate Mortgages

There's a legitimate debate about whether to prepay a mortgage at all. With inflation running at 2-3% annually, a fixed 6.5% mortgage has a "real" (inflation-adjusted) rate of ~3.5-4.5%.

However, the risk-free nature of mortgage prepayment (guaranteed 6.5% after-tax equivalent return) makes it competitive with many investment alternatives, especially when accounting for risk-adjusted returns.
